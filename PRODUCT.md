# Product

## Register

product

## Users

High school students (roughly 14–18). They're juggling 5–7 classes, extracurriculars, and an unstable schedule. They open Crew in three modes:

- **Planner mode** — usually on a school laptop or Chromebook, deciding when and with whom to study this week.
- **Pocket mode** — on a phone between classes, on the bus, in bed: receiving nudges, accepting an invite, joining a session at the last minute.
- **In-session mode** — actively running or attending a study session, either remote or co-located.

They are not "kids." They are capable, social, schedule-strapped, and deeply allergic to anything that looks like the LMS portal their school forces them to use.

## Product Purpose

Crew helps high schoolers schedule and run study sessions with the right people. The headline job is scheduling — matching is in service of finding the right session-mates, not the other way around.

Success looks like:

- A student opens the app, sees what's next, and acts in one tap (join, accept, reschedule).
- A new user finds and joins their first session within their first week without messaging anyone first.
- Students reach for Crew instead of group chats when planning study time.

## Brand Personality

Three words: **considered, quietly confident, respectful.**

- Voice is direct, warm, and unhurried — never condescending, never trying to sound like a teen, never reaching for energy it doesn't have.
- The product has personality (a clear voice, deliberate color, snappy motion), but personality lives in the small, well-made details, not in volume.
- Reference lane: the quiet confidence of Linear, Bear, and Things, applied to scheduling — calm enough that a 17-year-old at 11pm trusts it, sharp enough that they don't dismiss it as a school tool.

## Anti-references

What Crew explicitly should not look or feel like:

- **School LMS portals** (Canvas, Schoology, PowerSchool, Google Classroom admin views). Beige, dense, bureaucratic, designed for administrators and compliance — the single strongest anti-reference. Every design choice should feel like the opposite.
- **Cliché "edtech for kids"** — cartoon owls, balloon fonts, neon gradient backgrounds, "Great job, scholar!" copy. Talks down to teens.
- **Corporate SaaS dashboards** — navy header, white card grid, generic sans, "Welcome back, [Name]" greetings. Cold, adult, blends in.
- **Group-chat chaos** — endless scroll, no structure, everything equally loud. The thing Crew replaces, not the thing it imitates.

## Design Principles

1. **Treat them as capable.** No tutorials masquerading as features, no over-explanation, no condescending copy. If a 17-year-old would roll their eyes at it, cut it.
2. **Scheduling is the hero. Everything else stays out of its way.** Whatever the screen, the next session and the next decision should be the loudest things on it.
3. **One-tap from pocket.** Every primary action — join, accept, decline, reschedule — must work in one tap on a phone, even if the same flow expands on desktop.
4. **Anti-LMS by reflex.** When choosing between two visual options, pick the one that looks less like Canvas. Bold color over beige, expressive type over generic sans, generous spacing over dense tables.
5. **Quiet confidence over volume.** Where the brief used to call for energy in color, energy now lives in *considered details*: tight tracking on display type, snappy ease-out motion, varied spacing rhythm, a single deliberate green that earns its presence by appearing only on the action a student should take next. The product should feel alive without raising its voice.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- **WCAG AA baseline** — contrast, focus rings, keyboard navigation, semantic landmarks. Non-negotiable for a school-adjacent product.
- **`prefers-reduced-motion` respected** — all non-essential motion has a reduced-motion fallback. The product's energy must not become an accessibility tax.
- Touch targets ≥ 44px on mobile. Color is never the only carrier of meaning. Form errors are announced, not just shown.
- Designed to work on the modest hardware students actually own (school Chromebooks, mid-range Androids, older iPhones) on imperfect school Wi-Fi.
